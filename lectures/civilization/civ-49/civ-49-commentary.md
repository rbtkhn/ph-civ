## Layer 1 - Neutral Summary

The lecture begins the final section of the course on the Anglo-American Empire. Jiang argues that to understand Britain, the course must first compare Spain and the Dutch Republic: Spain receives New World wealth into a feudal Catholic monarchy, while the Dutch develop commercial and middle-class forms.

The Dutch Golden Age is presented through capitalism, corporations, trade, urban life, domestic interiors, art, and the psychological world of the middle class. The lecture treats Dutch society as a decisive predecessor to later British and American modernity.

The ending turns toward art, hypocrisy, taboo, and literature, especially the way middle-class domestic ideals generate forbidden desire and narrative tension. Those claims should remain distinct from the economic and political claims.

### Core Concepts Defined

- **Middle-class civilization:** a social world organized around commerce, property, domesticity, and respectability.
- **Commercial republic:** political and economic order driven by trade, credit, corporations, and urban elites.
- **Domestic interiority:** art and literature used to see the private life and contradictions of the middle class.
- **Imperial seedbed:** an earlier formation that supplies habits later amplified by Britain and America.
