## Layer 1 - Neutral Summary

The lecture begins by distinguishing Christian tradition from the course's reconstruction of early Christianity. It reviews Jesus, James, Paul, the poor of Jerusalem, Gnostic and mystery-cult alternatives, and the conditions under which Paul's smaller church could become dominant after the Jewish-Roman wars.

Constantine is then framed as the ruler who uses Christianity to consolidate imperial authority after civil war. The Council of Nicaea and the godhead become the core institutional solution: an equation-like doctrine that is difficult to personalize, debate, or combine with rival stories. In the lecture's argument, this creates monotheism as a new kind of reality-order.

The chapter then makes its broadest move by connecting monotheism to modernity. Money, science, and the nation-state are treated as modern successors to the godhead's logic: symbols become reality, rival realities are crowded out, and expansion becomes necessary to make the system true. These claims are powerful for course architecture but require strong final review before becoming settled analysis.

### Core Concepts Defined

- **Christian tradition**: the received narrative the lecture contrasts with its reconstruction of early Christian development.
- **Godhead**: the Nicaean formula treated as an equation-like doctrine that produces blind obedience and exclusion.
- **Monotheism as reality-order**: the lecture's claim that monotheism remakes what counts as real, not merely what counts as divine.
- **Symbols as reality**: the idea that money, nation, and scientific objectivity can become treated as reality itself.
- **Crowding out realities**: the process by which one totalizing reality suppresses spiritual, symbolic, and imaginative alternatives.
