## Layer 1 - Neutral Summary

The lecture asks why the Byzantine Empire exists, how it endured for more than a thousand years, and why it finally collapsed. Jiang first presents a conventional account: Constantine moved the capital east for strategic defense, access to wealthy provinces, and proximity to the Persian frontier.

The chapter then surveys Byzantine durability through Constantinople's geography, walls, sea defenses, Greek fire, the Hagia Sophia, Justinian, Belisarius, plague, and Ottoman conquest. It emphasizes the city as a center of trade, theology, and Roman-Greek inheritance.

Jiang then offers his own interpretation: Byzantium should be understood through Christianity, bureaucracy, multicultural cosmopolitanism, and the conversion of Roman republican energy into imperial administration. The lecture contrasts pagan and Christian worldviews and argues that bureaucracy first creates stability and prosperity, then hardens into conformity, censorship, and stagnation.
