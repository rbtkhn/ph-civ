## Layer 1 - Neutral Summary

The lecture continues the French Revolution sequence by moving from Rousseau as the philosopher-poet of revolution to Robespierre as the prophet who tries to lead the people into the promised land of reason. Jiang marks the central argument as provocative: Robespierre is read through a religious and sacrificial frame rather than only as a political operator.

The chapter reviews the social setting of the Revolution, including peasants, clergy, nobility, and the rising middle class. It treats revolution as a struggle to replace old hierarchies with a new system of virtue, reason, and political purity.

The ending frames the Terror and Robespierre downfall through sacrifice, pollution, guilt, and scapegoating. The in-review commentary should preserve the force of the lecture argument while keeping violence, religious analogy, and revolutionary causation carefully bounded.
