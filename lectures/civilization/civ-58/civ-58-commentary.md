## Layer 1 - Neutral Summary
The lecture opens the final three-class course sequence by defining the nation-state and presenting nationalism as one of the most powerful modern ideologies. Jiang argues that it solves religious, economic, and social-cultural pressures created by Protestantism, industrialization, bourgeois property, urbanization, mobility, alienation, and disenchantment.

The middle of the lecture contrasts Enlightenment and romantic sources of nationalism. French nationalism is tied to Rousseau, the general will, citizenship, rights, and the French Revolution, while German nationalism is tied to culture, language, Geist, romanticism, resistance to Napoleon, and the nation as a kind of church.

The later sections move into race science, eugenics, imperialism, Japan, World War I, fascism, totalitarianism, and Pax Americana. These claims set up Stalin and the American Empire, but they require firm guardrails around antisemitism, the Holocaust, race, eugenics, imperial violence, national cohesion, identity politics, immigration, and current political analogies.
