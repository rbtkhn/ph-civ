## Layer 1 - Neutral Summary
The lecture presents Stalin as an extreme world-historical actor whose rise should not have been expected: the Bolsheviks should not have triumphed, Stalin should not have beaten other Bolshevik leaders, and the Soviet Union should not have defeated Germany. Jiang frames Stalin through Nietzschean overman language and Russian revolutionary inheritance.

The middle sections trace Russian revolutionary politics, the secret police, Lenin, Trotsky, Bolshevik organization, criminal networks, and Stalin's rise through loyalty, silence, patronage, emotional intelligence, and institutional control. The lecture treats Stalin as different from priestly ideologues because he wanted power more than truth.

The final sections move into purges, show trials, World War II, Operation Barbarossa, lend-lease, Stalin's alleged strategic manipulation, nationalism, Mother Russia, and Putin analogies. These are among the highest-risk claims in the course and require explicit representation-not-endorsement and external review.
