# The Loneliness of Mad King Trump

## Part I: Full transcript

By the time you read this, it is possible that Trump has attacked Cuba or Iran or both. Trump canceled his weekend golf excursion to rush back to the White House.

On Thursday, many were anticipating a breakthrough in US-Iran negotiations. There was a Qatari delegation in Tehran, and the GCC, China, and Pakistan were applying diplomatic pressure. With American warships blockading Cuba, Trump’s golf partner Lindsey Graham tweeted out: “I believe the liberation of the wonderful people of Cuba from the clutches of communism is close at hand.”

Last week, the United States indicted Cuba’s 94-year old former leader Raul Castro, creating the legal justification for the American military to kidnap Castro as they had Maduro. Decapitating Cuba would remove Russian influence from the Western hemisphere, and that would be in line with the Donroe Doctrine. Trump would be the sort to prefer a quick and easy victory in Cuba over a drawn-out and uncertain round two with Iran.

A round two would be devastating for both sides. Running low of munitions and conscious of the American mid-terms, the Americans and Israelis would chose indiscriminate bombing of critical civilian infrastructure (power plants, oil refineries, railways, bridges, reservoirs, and desalination plants) to cause political chaos within Iran. The Iranians would response with ferocity:

With the Houthis, they could close down the Bab al-Mandab Strait. This would knock out another five percent of the world’s energy supply, accelerating the collapse of the global economy. If this were to happen, Saudi Arabia may be forced to attack the Houthis.

They could cut the underwater sea cables that runs through the Strait of Hormuz. This would mean that the UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, and Saudi Arabia would lose Internet access, and cause digital banking disruption as far as India.

The Iranians could choose to destroy the UAE, which has been the most aggressive of the GCC countries. They already consider the UAE an Israeli proxy, and the nation is fully exposed to Iranian retaliation.

Both the Americans and the Iranians chose tactical restraint in the first round. In the second, we should expect wanton destruction. Once the destruction starts, it is hard to imagine the possibility of another ceasefire.

It is becoming harder to imagine the possibility of our peace in our time. This morning, I woke up to some startling news. Trump’s Director of National Intelligence Tulsi Gabbard had announced her resignation, effective June 30th, in order to care for husband, who had been diagnosed with bone cancer. An anti-war Democrat, Gabbard was always an odd choice. She was kept in the dark when Trump ordered the attacks on both Venezuela and Iran. Her aides joke that their office stands for “Do Not Invite.”

Her deputy Joe Kent resigned in protest to the war in Iran, and she herself testified before Congress last March that she did not believe that Iran was building a nuclear weapon. There were rumors that Trump wanted to axe Gabbard after he had demoted Kristi Noem and fired Pam Bondi.

For some time now, I believed that Trump was a political genius of a strange sort, and I believed he would protect Gabbard to hedge against the hawks in his Cabinet. Gabbard was also spearheading an investigation into election tampering, an issue dear to Trump’s heart.

Many inside the Trump administration saw Gabbard as a threat from the start, and had knives out for her. Last year, Gabbard wanted to appoint Daniel Davis as her deputy. Davis had criticized Israeli atrocities in Gaza, and was skeptical of any military intervention abroad. The news of his appointment was leaked, creating a political furor and forcing Gabbard to withdraw her offer.

Last week, Congresswoman Anna Paulina Luna claimed that the CIA had raided Gabbard’s office, and seized her files on the JFK assassination and MK-Ultra project. Gabbard herself denied this, but the rumor must have made Trump question Gabbard’s competence. In theory, the CIA reports to Gabbard’s office — but there were rumors that the CIA was spying on her, and she had clashed with CIA director John Ratcliffe in the past. Trump might have asked for Gabbard’s resignation for the same reason that he had fired Pam Bondi, who could not secure an indictment against former FBI director James Comey. He probably saw both as too weak-willed, and lost confidence in them. The four Cabinet members that Trump has fired so far (Kristi Noem, Pam Bondi, Lori Chavez-DeRemer, and Tulsi Gabbard) are all women.

Gabbard’s departure follows the resignation of Amaryllis Fox Kennedy, another of Gabbard’s deputies skeptical of the war against Iran. This leaves only Elbridge Colby, the Under Secretary of War for Policy, as the last prominent Iran war skeptic in the Trump administration. He is the grandson of CIA director William Colby, and before I would be confident that he would have the political acumen to survive this purge.

But of late I have been very wrong about American politics. I was wrong to believe that Marjorie Taylor-Greene would fight to keep her Congressional seat. I was wrong to believe that Gabbard had the trust of Trump. I was wrong to believe Thomas Massie would win easily against his primary challenger. I see now the folly of trying to make sense of Trump’s madness.

Massie’s opponents spent over $30 million, making the Kentucky Republican primary the most expensive in American history. Three Zionist billionaires (Miriam Adelson, Paul Singer, and John Paulson) worked together to oust Massie, who had been for the release of the Epstein files as much as he had been against the Iran war. His opponent Ed Gallrein refused to debate Massie, and stayed a cipher throughout. Secretary of War Peter Hegseth flew to Kentucky to campaign against Massie. Ed Gallrein only won the Baby Boomer demographic, and that was enough to secure a ten-point victory.

The removal of Tulsi Gabbard and Thomas Massie will prove pyrrhic victories for Trump. Their downfall was not that they were disloyal, but that they were loyal to a fault, believing that Trump shared their principles. Those who have the most influence around Trump appreciate that he places subservience above loyalty, but that also means that everyone is a pawn that can be sacrificed at any moment. This is a lesson that Michael Cohen, who served Trump loyally from 2006 to 2018 as his fixer and personal lawyer, learned the hard way. Those who seem to have control of Trump have done so by being subservient sycophants around him, and directing his limited attention span to those who are not.

Given Trump’s track record, we can expect him to continue to purge those whom he felt has betrayed him. He may even target Tucker Carlson, who had single-handedly convinced him not to go to war against Iran in January 2020. Carlson is still tirelessly working to convince Trump of the folly of war when Trump has yet to forgive him for stymying his war efforts in 2020. Perhaps Trump believes he would have won the 2020 election had he gone to war against Iran after he ordered the assassination of Qasem Soleimani? (Given the way things have turned out, this would not be wrong.)

In mid-March, Trump responded to Carlson’s criticisms of the Iran war by calling him a “low-IQ person.” In mid-April, Carlson’s son left his White House position as JD Vance’s deputy press secretary. On his Substack, Ken Klippenstein has reported that Sebastian Gorka, the White House’s top counter-terrorism official, is considering charging Tucker Carlson a domestic terrorist. Carlson has been a loyal friend to Trump for many years, but Trump demands subservience, not loyalty. If Trump goes after Carlson, it may not break MAGA — but it would certainly break Trump.

Recently, I read Peter Thiel’s book Zero to One, and a particular passage has left an indelible impression on me:

Before execution, scapegoats were often worshipped like deities. The Aztecs considered their victims to be earthly forms of the gods to whom they were sacrificed. You would be dressed in fine clothes and feast royally until your brief reign ended and they cut your heart out. These are the roots of monarchy: every king was a living god, and every god a murdered king. Perhaps every modern king is just a scapegoat who has managed to delay his own execution.

Those who have the most sway over Trump have promised him a third term. They anoint him their king so that one day they may sacrifice him as their scapegoat.

Three more points before we end:

Next week, watch for Putin’s ferocious response to Ukraine’s bombing of a Russian college dormitory in Luhansk, killing at least six. Putin was visibly upset when talking about the incident. Look for him to expand operations in Ukraine.

Next week, also watch for Chinese President Xi Jinping’s visit to North Korea. He last visited in 2019. I believe the Korean peninsula could become the flashpoint for southeast Asia. The United States is pre-occupied with Cuba and Iran, and cannot afford a flare-up in southeast Asia.

Will Trump call Taiwan President Lai Ching-te? Trump has paused a $14 billion arms package for Taiwan, which China sees as a goodwill gesture. The two nations have already established a board of trade and a board of investment. There are rumors that China may invest one trillion dollars in America, which may be announced as the highlight of President Xi’s visit to America in September. Trump could derail this detente with one poorly timed phone call.
