# Countdown to Iran

America has positioned one-third of its naval assets into the Middle East, and the super-carrier Gerald Ford has crossed the Strait of Gibraltar en route to the Arabian Sea. Not since Operation Iraqi Freedom in 2003 has America assembled such “maximum lethality” – which suggests that the Americans are more interested in regime change than in negotiating leverage. It is ominous that Trump’s envoys are Jared Kushner and Steve Witkoff, two Zionists close to Netanyahu.

They also burned statues of Baal, broadcasting their belief that theirs is a war against the Great Satan.

It seems that the Iranian regime is on the precipice. Decades of sanctions have made Iran corrupt, fractured, and vulnerable. In January 2020, the Americans assassinated Qasem Soleimani without consequence. October 7th surprised the Iranians more than the Israelis, and they were powerless to help their proxies. The fall of the Assad regime in Syria opened up an air corridor for Israel to bomb Iran with impunity. In the 12-Day War, Israel assassinated many high-level Iranian officials and scientists, revealing the depth of Mossad penetration into the highest echelons of power. Last month, protests roared across Iran, and security forces killed thousands. Given how easily Delta Force swooped in to abduct Maduro on January 3rd, what fighting chance does the Ayatollah have?

From a military perspective, the most effective way to conduct regime change in Tehran is a multi-prong embargo and sabotage campaign to sap the morale of the Islamic Revolutionary Corps Guard Corps. Create chaos and conflict in Iran’s borderlands, and destroy the state’s capacity to deliver water and electricity to households. Wait for the death of 86-year old Khamenei, which would trigger a succession crisis.

In a world long lost to us, the American military practiced the Powell Doctrine, which argued that America should only participate in wars in which it had a clear strategic interest, in which the objectives were clear and limited, and in which it enjoyed overwhelming superiority as well as broad public support.

With Iran, America is embracing the Anti-Powell Doctrine:

How would America benefit strategically from toppling the Iranian theocracy?

What does America hope to accomplish militarily?

How could America prevent Iran from retaliating against GCC oilfields and American bases?

What fail-safes are in place to stop this war from spiraling out of control, forcing America to send ground troops to protect the Strait of Hormuz?

What counter-strategies do the American carrier strike groups have for Iran’s use of drones, ballistic missiles, and swarm submarines?

How will Trump explain to the American people why so many servicemen are dying – in a war that was never declared by Congress?

This blindness to the possibility of defeat is what the Greeks called “hubris,” the fatal disease of empire. What most analysts fail to appreciate is how the aggressions of Israel and America have made Iran more united and resilient. Yes, the 12-Day War killed many Iranian officials, but it exposed Mossad’s human intelligence networks in Tehran. Yes, the January protests rattled Tehran, but at the cost of the local underground infiltration and sabotage networks that Mossad had spent years and millions in cultivating.

Yes, the Delta Force kidnapping of Maduro made for great television – but it has made the American military more arrogant and risk-tolerant. Peter Hegseth’s purge of dissenters within the Pentagon has made the military more determined to fight wars, but less capable of battlefield resilience and innovation. A better indicator of the American military’s true state is its performance against the Houthis in Operation Prosperity Guardian. Against an inferior but resilient enemy, the Americans chose to retreat, pretend the war did not happen, and never talk about it again.

The great unknown is whether Israel will use tactical nuclear weapons, as some have speculated. Yes, Israel is suspected of testing a nuke recently, but I do not believe Israel will commit any of its ninety nukes in this war. First, Israel’s Samson Option calls for nukes to be used against the world if its existence is threatened. To use nukes tactically would demand a fundamental re-wiring of Israeli military doctrine. Second, America will take the initiative in this war, and as such Iran will calibrate its response against all American assets and allies in the region.

Third, for Israel to achieve the Greater Israel Project, it needs America out of the Middle East. Israel does not benefit from a quick decisive American victory; it benefits from a sustained, drawn-out conflict that fractures Iran into ethnic enclaves, and plunges America into civil war.
