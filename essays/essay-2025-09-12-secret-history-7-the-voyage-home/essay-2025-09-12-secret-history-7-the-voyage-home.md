# Secret History #7: The Voyage Home

## Part I: Full transcript

I first read Homer’s *Odyssey* the summer after my first year at Yale. I had applied to Directed Studies, which was Yale’s Great Books program. After I failed to get in, I took it upon myself to master the Great Books, and I failed at that as well.

Even to this day I do not know how I got into Yale on a full scholarship. It was like winning the lottery, and it was just as disorienting and traumatizing. I was a poor Chinese immigrant who went to a public high school in Toronto. I did not have the smooth vocabulary and refined habits of my classmates.

I was an English major, and I did not know you were supposed to prepare for class by doing the assigned reading. (Why should I have to do the reading – wasn’t that what the professor was for?) When I did attempt to read *The Canterbury Tales* or *Paradise Lost* I fell asleep.

I slept my way through Yale. It was not possible to fail out of Yale (I got a C+ in physics class, which would have been an F anywhere else). It was depressing being there. I had no friends, which defeated the purpose of being there. The Yale brochure I received prior to my arrival promised me that there was a niche for anyone. Well, I proved I wasn’t “anyone.” I couldn’t find a roommate, so I was assigned to the attic of my residential college. It was called Weir Hall, and I wished I had made that up.

I developed Imposter Syndrome, and was glad to graduate. But Yale stayed in me, and I wanted to succeed, without trying and without waiting. It did not matter if I was working as a teacher or a journalist or a United Nations official. I couldn’t control my anger, got into fights with those who wanted to help me, and burned all my bridges with glee. By my late twenties, I was jobless, penniless, and playing Starcraft from dusk to dawn in my parents’ basement back in Toronto.

When my parents kicked me out, I went to Beijing to drink my days away, and fall in love with a woman with a history of depression and drugs. One night, I sleepwalked naked into a Beijing blizzard, and that snapped me awake.

I came to recognize that Yale had instilled in me an arrogance borne out of insecurity, a competitiveness borne out of fear, and an ambition borne out of self-loathing. Yale had MKUltra’ed me, and I was determined to “de-program” myself.

I took up stand-up comedy to turn my personal trauma into joy for the world. I did sky-diving, rock-climbing, and Brazilian jiu-jitsu to control my fear and anger. I learned to farm, cook, and play the piano to center myself. I learned to laugh at myself, to respect the talent and hard work of others, and to see the beauty in my own ugly humanity.

Because the universe is mysterious but just, when I learned to accept who I was I met the love of my life. She was happy and healthy, and I was sure that she would run away screaming from me when she found out more about my past. I told her everything, and she stayed. We got married, and had three kids.

I became a high school teacher to support my growing family, and because I was teaching the Great Books to my students I picked up the *Odyssey* again. This time I could see its beauty and truth.

The horrors of the Trojan War traumatized Odysseus, and he is trapped on both Calypso’s Island as well as in his PTSD. The Gods free Odysseus of Calypso, and he slowly makes his way back home. It’s been twenty years since he’s last seen Penelope, and he’s afraid of her suitors as well as his PTSD so he disguises himself. Slowly, the love of family heals him, and this rekindled love gives him the strength and courage to reveal himself, and kill all of Penelope’s suitors.

My trauma cannot be feared and forgotten because it has become who I am. I no longer regret my life, even though it is filled with tragedy and trauma. It was all worth it because it led me to who I am today, and every day I’m thankful for my wife and three children.

The Great Books do hold the truth and beauty of the universe. But they cannot be studied at Yale, and they should not be. They will reveal themselves to us when we’re ready to understand them.

I spent six months studying Dante’s *Divine Comedy* pointlessly. The poetry eluded me. I watched dozens of YouTube lectures, and I became more confused. I even read Cliff Notes. It all seemed hopeless. I could never understand Dante.

One day, I prayed to Dante before going to bed, and closed my eyes. When I opened my eyes I knew that he had answered me. The truth was not out there in a book or a YouTube lecture or in the mind of a professor. It was inside my own heart.

That’s when I understood that there was a plan, after all. The tragic and traumatic contours of my life had now made me complete and whole. If I truly wanted to love, I needed to suffer. And if I truly wanted to understand Dante and Homer, I needed to love.

What did I learn from reading Homer and Dante? Love will take us home because love is our home.
