# The Empire Goes to War

## Part I: Full transcript

It looks like we’re getting World War Three for Christmas this year.

Here are three headlines from today’s Drudge Report:

- [USA Preps Military Strikes in Venezuela](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/rcna233734)
- [Europeans Privately Tell Russia They’re Ready to Down Jets](https://www.msn.com/en-us/news/world/ar-AA1Njwgc)
- [New Details on Hegseth Unusual Mass Gathering of Top Brass](https://www.msn.com/en-us/news/us/ar-AA1Nibmf)

All three are disturbing, especially Secretary of War Peter Hegseth’s calling for a meeting of his top military officers. It is confusing why these generals have to be at one place at one time. Such an event has not happened in recent memory. Many point out that the Pentagon has the best video-conferencing technology, and it’s risky to have the empire’s top managers away from their command posts all at once. This has fueled speculation that America is about to go to war.

Details are emerging that the meeting agenda will be focused on cost-cutting. The military is bloated, America too indebted, and Washington dysfunctional. If that is the agenda (and I think it is), then America will definitely go to war.

Let me explain why with a story. In October 2013, I was in Toronto (unemployed, as usual), and I wanted to visit friends in New York. I drove down to the border, and I was detained by American customs officers. They inspected my car, and interrogated me. Because I was unemployed without any property, they accused me of attempting to claim welfare in the United States (because everyone knows the American safety net is far superior to the Canadian one). It took many hours of negotiation before they permitted me into the United States, but only after I was put on a ten-year list in which I’d always have to submit to secondary questioning whenever I enter.

As a Canadian citizen, I was shocked by the ordeal. But every Canadian that day was interrogated by American customs. That day was the first day of the 2013 government shutdown, and the customs officers were afraid and anxious of being made redundant. I explained to one that I was taking a year off from work, and he responded that his mortgage meant he couldn’t take one day off.

You would think four-star generals would not mind retiring to their grand mansions and generous pensions, but they would very much. A President of the United States is a king, and a four-star general is an emperor. The general always has a G5 plane waiting on the tarmac to ferry him to any of the military’s 194 golf courses scattered around the world. In the different theaters of the American empire, it is the generals who wield true power. In a time of war or emergency, the American commanding officer assumes direct command over South Korea’s military, something that has long irritated South Koreans’ democratic sensibilities.

In a time of peace, the number of four-star generals has shot up, due to the logic of bureaucratic expansionism (if there’s no reason to exist, expansion becomes the reason). Today, there are almost forty four-star generals who command 1.32 million combat soldiers. During World War II, America had seven four-star generals for 16 million combat soldiers.

It makes sense to downsize the top-heavy American military. The problem is that cost-cutting incentivizes the generals to start wars to protect their privileges. And that’s exactly the point.

A week ago, if Trump were to propose a war against Russia or Iran or Venezuela or the Mexican drug cartels or transgender terrorists his generals —who achieved their status by being the consummate bureaucrats — would have demanded caution. With the threat of downsizing looming over them like the sword of Damocles, they will be the first to scream out: “Damn the torpedoes! Full speed ahead!”

But if an empire must go to war it has already lost the war. Yes, America could easily conquer and colonize Venezuela. But that has always been true, and the empire chose not to because it’s a pain in the ass. As the British taught the Americans, it’s better to co-opt local elites, and use them to pillage a nation’s resources. If you invade a country and implant your own administrators, you insult the people’s dignity, and encourage them to rebel. More problematic, this sense of indignity might spread throughout South America, forcing local elites to rebel against the empire, or face insurrections themselves. The moment the American empire goes to war is the moment it digs its own grave.

So why is Trump doing this? Because the moment the American empire goes to war is the moment it digs its own grave.

When it comes to Trump’s intentions for 2028, there have been two telling moments in the past month.

On August 18th, a few days after his summit with Putin, Trump met with Zelenskyy in the White House, and when asked by a reporter why Ukraine has not had elections in the past three and a half years Zelenskyy explained that national security came first in wartime. This seemed like a revelation to Trump, who asked Zelenskyy to confirm the concept. “You like this idea?” Zelenskyy laughed.

A couple of days ago Trump met with Erdogan in the White House, and he quipped to reporters that his Turkish friend knew “all about rigged elections.”

Here’s what Trump learned in 2020: The circus that is American democracy is the mechanism by which the deep state asserts control. If he allows for elections to take place the deep state will win. For Trump, it is better to take the empire to war than to allow it to rig another election. In the ensuing civil conflict, his MAGA movement has the best chance of triumphing. In this way, Trump will fulfill Oswald Spengler’s prophecy that a Caesar will arise to smash to pieces the kakistocracy that is America today.

This is the grand irony underlying Hegseth’s meeting. Those generals and the deep state permitted Trump to become president with the intention of scapegoating him for imperial over-reach. They believed that they could control him as they did in his first term when his cabinet was staffed with generals and longtime deep state operatives. Recall how Mark Milley, whom Trump appointed Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, called the Chinese to tell them to ignore Trump’s bellicose rhetoric — an act that suggested to the Chinese that Trump had no real power, and which could be interpreted as “treasonous” in a court of law.

In his second term, Trump promises to turn the tables on those generals who had mocked him in his first, and manipulate them into starting wars that are doomed to fail, and which they must take the blame. The ultimate genius of Trump is that his enemies remain blind to the method in his madness, which allows Trump to play them against each other.

Trump seems to be telling his generals: “Fight now to remain emperors — in your struggle against each other I’ll no longer be your king but your God.”
