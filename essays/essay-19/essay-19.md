# The Limits of Pax Judaica

## Part I: Full transcript

The world seemed on the brink. In late September, Netanyahu spoke at the United Nations, and visited the White House. He warned that Iran had nukes capable of striking New York and Miami. American air fuel tankers flew to Qatar, which was what happened right before the onset of the 12-day War.

Then at the eleventh-hour Trump pulled the world back from the precipice. Negotiations in Egypt led to Hamas and Netanyahu’s war cabinet agreeing to a ceasefire.

Next week, Hamas will release the 48 remaining Israeli hostages, and Israel will release over two thousand Palestinians. The IDF will pull back from half of Gaza, and aid trucks can flow unfettered.

This will be the third cease-fire since the war broke out in October 2023. No one thinks that Trump will achieve peace in the Middle East for the first time in three thousand years. There is hope that the senseless slaughter will stop.

Trump wants his Nobel Peace Prize after all. Tony Blair will lead an international team to govern Gaza. Then there is how Jared Kushner has re-surfaced.

Perhaps this was always the plan. International bankers have long salivated at Gaza. It is estimated that there is a rich reservoir of oil and natural gas lurking off the shores of Gaza. The proposed Ben-Gurion Canal – which will challenge the supremacy of the Suez Canal – must run through Gaza. At a talk at his alma mater Harvard, Jared Kushner discussed how Gaza can be valuable waterfront property. Earlier this year, Trump shared an AI-generated video of Gaza as the Riviera of the Middle East.

Now that the IDF has flattened Gaza, Trump and his surrogate Kushner no longer have to worry about eviction, usually the costliest and trickiest part of any real estate deal. The Gulf Arab nations have committed themselves to re-building Gaza. That means building high-rises and printing welfare checks to imprison the Palestinians in a gilded cage, while the waterfront is turned into villas for the world’s wealthiest. War, destruction, and famine have unified and hardened the Palestinians, and now safety, stability, and riches will divide and conquer them.

There is a problem with Trump Gaza though, and it has to do with why Netanyahu agreed to the ceasefire. There is talk that the IDF is over-extended, that the Israeli people are fed up with war, and that Netanyahu is planning to strike Iran. The real cause is that Israel is losing the war against Hamas.

Very few would consider this live-streamed genocide a war. Hospitals were bombed, journalists were targeted, and the people systemically starved. How was that a war? As Clausewitz pointed out, war is about breaking an enemy’s will to fight. As the Palestinians starved, their faith armored them. As the Israelis killed, their resolve traumatized them.

It will always be a military secret as to how many IDF reservists suffer from depression and PTSD, how many cannot sleep, and how many have killed themselves. I suspect that these numbers are significant. As I have pointed out, the Israeli slaughter of Gaza can best be described as a ritual child sacrifice meant to galvanize the soldiers and the population for the tribulations ahead. This practice worked well for the Aztecs who were surrounded by enemies, and for the Romans and the Carthaginians in their war for the Mediterranean. The problem with shooting yourself up with too much adrenaline is you need an enemy to fight – otherwise, you risk ripping your eyes out. Peace can only promise hell for Israel.

In May 2012, when I was a program director at Peking University High School, the Technion (Israel’s MIT) invited us to visit. The Israeli embassy rushed our visas, and the Technion’s President and most famous professors taught classes specifically for us. The Israelis shocked us with how open, curious, and introspective they were. My students and I cried after our visit to the Holocaust Museum in Jerusalem – one of the most profound experiences of my life.

What do open, curious, and introspective people do when they have to confront the Holocaust they’ve committed?

One of the best films to have come out of Israel is the 2008 animated documentary Waltz with Bashir: a Lebanon War Story. In it, the filmmaker Ari Folman has recurring nightmares, and in his search for demons he discovers his heart of darkness. He had served in the IDF during its 1982 invasion of Lebanon. The IDF was complicit in sectarian violence in Beirut, which led to the massacre of thousands of Palestinian families. The animated film ends with live footage of the aftermath of the massacre, with widows and mothers crying at the loss of their husbands and children – an event that Ari Folman had witnessed personally. The film is a testament to how the evil of war traumatizes and traps the soul.

Israel has crossed the Rubicon, and there can be no turning back. Itamar Ben Gvir and Bezalel Smotrich – whose support Netanyahu requires to remain in power – oppose the peace settlement, and the most extreme elements of the IDF will refuse to honor the ceasefire. They will be cast as the villains, but the raw truth is that the nation of Israel cannot survive an honest look in the mirror.

The Israelis will achieve their Pax Judaica, and that is perhaps their destiny. But it must come at the cost of their soul, which is perhaps also their destiny.
