# Blowback Venezuela

## Part I: Full transcript

In the early morning of January 3rd, America’s elite Delta Force roused Nicolas Maduro and his wife from their sleep, the climax of a daring raid in which low-flying Chinook helicopters ran past fast-asleep anti-aircraft batteries in Caracas.

Secretary of State and National Security Advisor Marco Rubio tells us that Maduro and his wife will be indicted in the Southern District of New York. Attorney General Pam Bondi tells us that he will face charges of “Narco-Terrorism Conspiracy, Cocaine Importation Conspiracy, Possession of Machineguns and Destructive Devices, and Conspiracy to Possess Machineguns and Destructive Devices against the United States.”

The Freemason Empire of America believes the number “thirteen” has cosmological significance. That is why on January 3rd, 1989, the Empire apprehended Manuel Noriega, the Panamanian strongman who was also a CIA asset. On January 3rd, 2020, the Empire assassinated Qasem Soleimani, Iran’s de facto ambassador in the Middle East.

It’s still early, and questions abound. How was it possible for the United States to capture so easily Maduro? His security was provided by the Cubans, who depend on Venezuela for oil — what happened to his security team? The Venezuelan military offered no resistance, and the Americans suffered no casualties. Was there a secret deal between Maduro and Trump? If there was an arrangement of sorts, why did the Americans have to bomb the Mausoleum of Hugo Chavez? China’s Latin American envoy met with Maduro for three hours before his kidnapping – was this a deliberate insult to China?

The most important question is how the government and people of Venezuela will respond to such a brazen attack on their national sovereignty. Maduro is gone, and his vice-president Delcy Rodriguez (who has been sworn in as president) promises to resist. Her brother heads the Parliament, and their Marxist father was tortured to death by the US-supported Venezuela military dictatorship. The Interior Minister Diosdado Cabello Rondon – a leader of another powerful political faction within the Chavismo movement – was rumored to have been killed in the strikes, but he has appeared in public rallying the military and militia.

How are we to understand Trump’s actions? Let us give him the benefit of the doubt, and assume there is a Grand Strategy here. If we consider Trump’s National Security Strategy to be the broad framework, then the attack against Venezuela has three main objectives:

Establish the Trump Corollary to the Monroe Doctrine. Trump is using Venezuela as a warning to Mexico, Colombia, Brazil, Nicaragua, and Cuba. Resistance is futile. America has the will and the ability to decapitate all resistance outside the rules of engagement with “maximum lethality” in Secretary of War Peter Hegseth’s words.

Strangle China. China imports eighty percent of Venezuela’s crude oil, and obtains one-third of its overseas food supply (specifically soybeans) from Brazil. Resource-hungry China needs to trade with resource-rich South America, and has built a mega-port in Peru. China is especially desperate for copper, lithium, and silver – and the Western Hemisphere is the world’s leading producer of all three. By demonstrating that America controls the Western Hemisphere, Trump gives himself leverage in trade negotiations when he visits Beijing in April.

Prepare for War Against Iran. Trump has warned that America will protect protestors in Iran, the strongest sign that he is looking for any pretext to attack Iran again. A war in the Middle East would cut off oil supplies to China, South Korea, and Japan – forcing all three to source their oil mainly from the Western Hemisphere.

If this were his Grand Strategy, Trump could simply have negotiated with Maduro, who was open to American trade and investment.

For the longest time, I wanted to believe that there was a certain method to Trump’s madness, and I struggled with his actions in the Caribbean. I was wrong to believe that Trump would not attack Venezuela, and I was wrong to believe that Trump was not intent on regime change. I am forced to re-evaluate my understanding of Trump, and my working hypothesis now is that Trump’s decision-making is driven mainly by one question: “Does this look good on TV?”

Any television executive could tell you that the Venezuela raid made for great television. Maduro’s trial will also make for great TV, especially when he spills the beans on Venezuela’s complicity in the drug trade.

There are certain problems with great television though. First, no one (especially the people of Venezuela with their memory of Yankee imperialism) likes being cast as disposable extras. Trump will now find it harder to negotiate a deal for Venezuela’s resources. Second, TV ratings ought not to be a factor in geo-politics. The Ukrainians dominated social media, but their counter-offensives doomed them on the battlefield. Unfortunately, they don’t know that because focusing on TV ratings trap them in an alternate reality.

Third – and this is something that Trump understands best – TV ratings are addictive, and he’ll order more daring military adventures to capture the short attention span of Americans. Expect more sensational military pageantry in South America, Africa, and the Middle East in 2026.

And Trump’s war in Venezuela has just begun. We will never know if Maduro was a CIA asset, but he certainly played the part. In response to Trump’s bullying he led peace rallies, and begged to negotiate. He was an ineffectual leader who diminished the Chavismo movement.

Delcy Rodriguez — an experienced politician with street cred — will prove a tougher adversary.
