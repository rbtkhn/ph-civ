# The Secret History of the World #1: The Cost of Money

## Part I: Full transcript

This week I started a new course, in which I attempt to explain the present and predict the future so that we can connect the past, and reveal the secret history of the world.

I introduced the course by referencing Immanuel Kant. He taught us that rather than passive observers we are active participants in reality. When we perceive reality, we manipulate it in a way that allows us to process it. In other words, we imagine reality.

Dante taught us that that was God’s ultimate gift to us. Because His love for us was unconditional we have the freedom to create our own realities. He will never judge us for what we choose.

A series of historical accidents as well as the manipulations of the powerful have led us to imagine for ourselves a Hell that we call Heaven.

Let us look closely an example of this: money.

To understand how money works, let us do a thought experiment. Let us pretend we are a bank, and we’re able to attract one million dollars in deposits. We then lend one million dollars to an entrepreneur to open a restaurant. How much money do we now have?

The answer ought to be zero, but it is actually two million. Unbeknownst to the public, banks have the power to print money. In an interview with Tucker Carlson the German economist Richard Werner explains that banks always had this power.

In the beginning, banks took gold, and offered receipts (the promise to return the gold at any time), which was traded among merchants and became money in effect. When the banks lent money, they didn’t hand over their gold holdings. For the sake of convenience, they gave receipts instead, thus printing money.

This system worked well most of the time, but was always at the risk of sudden collapse. If too many people withdrew at the same time (a bank run) or if a king defaulted on a loan, the bank risked bankruptcy.

Over time, banks around the world recognized their optimal strategy was to band together. Collectively, they would have the global resources to withstand local bank runs.

As well, if a king defaulted, the banking cartel could sponsor a rival claimant to the throne. This is what led to the downfall of Richard III of England (who refused to pay back the Italian banking cartel), and the ascendancy of the House of Tudor. Henry VII let loose his tax collectors on the people so that he could repay the Italians, and then his son Henry VIII beheaded the tax collectors to establish his legitimacy and popularity with the people.

Bankers are always attempting to mitigate risks, and while they could overthrow monarchs they preferred not to. The ideal situation would be for these private banks to become embedded in the nation-state itself, so that the “people” would be the ultimate guarantors of loans to the state. This is what led to the creation of the Bank of England in 1694. It was an investor’s wet dream. It was a private central bank backed and protected by the Parliament of an island fortress. Dutch gold (accumulated from the extremely profitable East Indies spice trade) flooded England, allowing it to defeat all its continental rivals (including Napoleon).

The Bank of England would seed the British empire. Because the Bank of England could absorb wealth from anywhere, England pioneered a system in which local elites could be co-opted in helping to exploit their own people and steal local resources (the classic example is India). This system still continues today, and helps us understand why decades after independence the nations of South America and Africa and Asia are still mired in malfunction and corruption.

***

If banks could just print money, why do we have poverty, inequality, and debt?

What mainstream economists teach us is that it’s because resources are scarce, and they are allocated to those who can make best use of it. As maverick economists (who just happen to be honest) Richard Werner and Michael Hudson explain so well, this is not true. Money can be created out of thin air, and if society so chooses poverty, inequality, and debt can vanish with the tap of a computer keystroke.

We do not do this because money is first and foremost an illusion designed to fool us into working hard (and thus creating real wealth). Poverty, inequality, and debt exist to force us to believe that money has real value when it does not.

But sometimes too much easy money is generated into the system, making people lazy and complacent. At this point, the central banks may engineer a fiscal crisis (a stock market crash or a recession) to wipe out excess money from the system. Now and then, following a crisis of faith in the system, central banks may create a war (for example, World War I) to destroy wealth and reduce the population so that people can be motivated again to work hard for their masters.

Here’s the main takeaway: If central banking sounds like a sophisticated system of slavery, it might be because it is.
