## Law-discovery answer (draft)

**Question:** How does imperial machinery acquire a literary soul?

**Working answer:** Rome's arc is not Greek-style imaginative founding but **civic-military absorption** first, **myth-as-infrastructure** second. Open citizenship and cohesion-discipline-devotion explain survival through Hannibal (`civ-14`). Mediterranean victory intensifies land, debt, and triumph politics until Caesar makes **self-narration** a regime technology (`civ-15`). Assassination crystallizes myth; Octavian converts will, legality, and caution into Augustan settlement — republican surfaces, imperial substance (`civ-16`). Augustus then needs a **state epic**: Virgil's Aeneid inverts Homeric love/infinity into piety/obedience and Roman destiny (`civ-17`, `gb-08`). Imperial machinery acquires its literary soul when conquest outruns republican grammar and poetry is commissioned to naturalize the new order.

## Roman grammar (mechanism spine)

1. **Citizenship-manpower** — open body, cohesion triad; Pyrrhus/Punic tests (`civ-14`).
2. **Crisis survival** — Cannae as formative catastrophe; Carthage endgame (`civ-14`).
3. **Mythmaker** — Caesar joins narrative to action; commentaries as self-fashioning (`civ-15`).
4. **Will-heir settlement** — Octavian vs Antony; Actium; Augustan republican theater (`civ-16`).
5. **Literary soul** — Aeneas myth, Homer–Virgil contrast, empire as memory system (`civ-17`, `gb-08`).

## Chapter sections

### civ-14

Italy/Mediterranean power map; Roman rise via manpower and **open citizenship** (not Greek cultural brilliance). Pyrrhus, First Punic War, Hannibal/Cannae as supreme crisis; cohesion-discipline-devotion triad. Brutus and anti-tyranny myth; Greek/Carthaginian/Roman character contrast (lecture shorthand). Carthage destruction closes Punic arc; victory's afterlife previews Caesar. **Downstream:** post-146 crisis (`civ-15`).

### civ-15

Caesar as **mythmaker** — motives, success, assassination framed around narrative control. Post-146 land/debt/triumph economy; Gaul/Britain as self-commentary. Triumvirate, civil war, clemency, personality cult; reform as **identity threat** to republican self-image. **Downstream:** will and heir (`civ-16`).

### civ-16

Caesar's will → liberators' wars → Second Triumvirate → Philippi → Actium → 27 BCE. Antony's self-defeat (Cleopatra, Parthia, Caesar imitation); Octavian's heirship and propaganda. Pomerium/Senate taboos explain assassination surprise; Augustan settlement preserves republican form. Adoptive succession after Tiberius flagged as later weakness. **Downstream:** state epic need (`civ-17`).

### civ-17

Augustus: Praetorian Guard, professional army, Egypt treasury. **Aeneas myth** and Aeneid as Augustan instrument. Homer (love, imagination, infinity) vs Virgil (piety, obedience, eternity, end-of-history framing). Literature as civilizational identity; Egypt eternity preview (`civ-18`). Christianity inheritance bridge (forward). **Pairs:** `gb-08`.
