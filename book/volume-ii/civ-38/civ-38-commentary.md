## Layer 1 - Neutral Summary

The lecture turns to China as a place where the course models should be tested rather than merely applied to Europe. Jiang begins from China as a civilization that produced the compass, paper, printing, gunpowder, major state forms, and deep intellectual traditions, then asks why creativity seemed to decline after the Song period.

The chapter moves quickly through Chinese civilizational formation: the Yellow River, population pressure, war, fortified settlements, the Warring States, the Hundred Schools, Confucianism, Daoism, and Legalism. These are framed through open cooperative competition and the oceanic-currents model rather than as isolated facts.

The later emphasis is bureaucratic order. Jiang treats Confucianism and the imperial examination system as mechanisms that legitimate and reproduce bureaucratic power, then uses the Ming examination story to argue that the center values stability and local control more than fairness in the abstract.
