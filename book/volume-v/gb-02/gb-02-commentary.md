## Layer 1 - Neutral Summary

The lecture opens with an imagination exercise on Achilles to show how great books make fictional characters feel real. It introduces Bloom's frame that to be human is to hear yourself speak — consciousness as imaginative dissociation. Book 1's council between Agamemnon and Achilles is read as a fight over narrative control, not mere insult exchange. Every Iliad character, including Nestor and observers, is treated as having independent interior life.

The central conflict is framed as struggle over who controls the story — face, sympathy, and suicidal stubbornness. Homer is presented as a mystery: one mind constructing a universe of real people, with archetype and universal-consciousness theories offered. Prophet-truth is distinguished from fortune-telling; moral prediction is eternal. Greek intellectuals including Plato and Thucydides are said to operate inside Homer's universe, with Thucydides read as Homeric speech-world history.
