**GLENN DIESEN:** Welcome back to the program. We are here today with Professor Jiang, who is very renowned not just for his academic achievements but also for his very popular channels — and I’ll make sure to leave links in the description.

**JIANG XUEQIN:** In my analysis of world history, when civilizations rise there are three factors at play.

The first is energy — this sort of hunger of the people to rise up and make their place in the world.

The second is openness — how willing are you to learn from others? How willing are you to reflect on your mistakes and improve on them?

The third is cohesion — do you identify as a people and work together to coordinate in order to achieve larger goals?

You look at all great empires — they all started off as these borderland tribes that, because of energy, openness, and cohesion, were able to dominate their part of the world. This includes the Athenians, the Persians, the Macedonians, the Romans, the Americans, the British, and today I would argue that the new empire coming onto the world stage is Pax Judea — Israel. Among the Israelis we see energy, openness, and cohesion.

**JIANG XUEQIN:** That’s a great point. Social cohesion is a huge problem in the Western world, especially in America. So the question then is: why is this happening? Why is there so much social and political polarization?

For that, let’s look at three theories.

The first is Peter Turchin. He proposed the idea of elite overproduction. He looked at the Roman civil wars, the French Revolution — all these times of political tumult — and he identifies elite overproduction as the main factor leading to civil war. His reasoning is that the world isn’t divided between rich and poor. It’s divided between the “have-a-lot” and the “have-some-but-want-more” — between the upper nobility and the lower nobility. The problem is that as society increases in wealth, there are more elites but only a limited number of positions of power. So these elites compete with each other for these limited positions.

You can argue that’s exactly what’s happening in the United States right now, where you have these Republican oligarchs (expressed by big tech — Peter Thiel, Elon Musk, etc.) versus the financial elite (represented by the Democratic Party). So you can almost see that the second Trump administration is really a civil war between these different oligarchs in America.

The second theory comes from Thomas Piketty in his book *Capital in the Twenty-First Century*. He proposes the idea that over time capitalism naturally leads to a rentier economy, where if you just engage in speculation you make more money than if you actually engage in productive labor. If you put your money in the stock market you might see about 5% annual returns. If you open a restaurant or start a factory you might see 2% annual returns. So everyone is going to put their money in speculative capital. That leads to the over-financialization of society and eventually to financial collapse because no one is producing any real wealth.

The third theorist is Oswald Spengler, who theorized that all civilizations are organic and have organic life cycles — meaning you’re born, you rise up, you mature, then you die. This is just a natural life cycle and there’s nothing you can do about it.

If you look at these three theories and apply their framework to America, America is really in a lot of trouble — and not just America but the Western world. How do you deal with elite overproduction? The only way is through revolution, civil war, or some cataclysmic social destruction where most of the elite is eliminated so that there’s more social mobility in America. Peter Turchin tells us we’re screwed.

Then you look at Thomas Piketty and you have the monopolization of capital, the consolidation of capital in America where now only a few companies account for like a third of stock market wealth. That’s very problematic because as monopolies they don’t have to create value — all they have to do is maintain their position. That’s why we see this artificial intelligence bubble happening in the United States, which is very perilous and precarious for the American economy. You’re putting all this money into these data centers producing a product — AI — that has no actual social benefit and which could threaten to destroy millions of jobs.

Oswald Spengler tells us that societies die just like people, and maybe for the sake of humanity that’s a very good thing.

So if you look at these three theories, it’s very hard to figure out a way out of the social decline that the Western world is facing right now.
