# Ten Axioms of My Historical Method

History is best read through durable civilizational forms, legitimacy
structures, symbolic memory, and recurring pressure patterns rather than
through isolated events alone. The aim is not to dissolve events into structure
or persons into systems, but to identify the level at which historical
explanation becomes most intelligible and most predictive.

## 1. Civilization is the primary unit

States, dynasties, parties, and movements matter, but they usually sit inside
longer civilizational continuities. If the deeper grammar of memory, order,
authority, and symbolic life is missed, the narrative will become too short and
too shallow to explain what is actually happening.

## 2. Legitimacy is deeper than policy

Policy explains surface motion; legitimacy explains why a political or
institutional order can endure, command loyalty, and survive strain. When a
civilization or regime enters visible decline, legitimacy exhaustion is usually
the deeper hinge through which other failures accelerate.

## 3. Religion is a civilizational grammar of meaning and order

Religion should not be reduced to belief alone or dismissed as mere elite
instrument. At civilizational scale it binds metaphysics, ritual, authority,
moral order, and collective self-understanding into a usable grammar for life.

## 4. Literature is a civilization remembering itself

At its highest level literature is not decoration but memory. Great works store
interiority, aspiration, loss, archetype, and symbolic self-recognition in a
form that can outlast regimes and outtravel institutions.

## 5. Great books are evidence, not ornament

Texts belong in historical work as civilizational evidence. They show what a
people could imagine, justify, revere, fear, and remember, which makes them
indispensable for chapter design, comparison, and law-discovery rather than
optional enrichment.

## 6. Empire is judged by the order it can sustain

The deepest test of empire is not its size but the quality of order it can
carry without self-consuming. The real question is whether an empire can
organize diversity, authority, continuity, and legitimacy across distance and
strain.

## 7. Leaders matter through structures

Singular figures can redirect history, but only after asking what structure
made them possible and why their moment could bear them. Great-man history
usually mistakes the visible carrier for the deeper condition that produced and
empowered the carrier.

## 8. Modernity is recomposition, not pure rupture

Modernity should usually be read as a recombination of older religious,
political, literary, and scientific forms under new pressure conditions. This
guards against both naive progress stories and crude claims that the modern
world appeared ex nihilo.

## 9. Prediction depends on identifying the operative level

Historical prediction becomes possible when the analyst identifies the level at
which recurring forms, legitimacy structures, and pressure mechanics are
actually operating. Prediction usually fails when event noise, rhetoric, or
personality drama is mistaken for the deeper layer that is doing the real work.

## 10. History is a struggle over form and meaning

History is neither simply progress nor simply decline. It is an ongoing contest
over what forms of order, memory, legitimacy, and human possibility can be
built, sustained, lost, or transformed under pressure.

## Operational Use

These axioms are meant to guide real design choices:

- structure a Volume I sequence around civilizational law-discovery rather than
  flat chronology
- decide whether a text is evidence or decoration
- compare civilizations through internal grammar before output metrics
- preserve contradiction in archives as evidence of fracture or transition
- keep predictive history from collapsing into analogy, punditry, or event
  chasing

They are also compatible with the existing two-volume PH architecture:

- Volume I discovers civilizational forms and laws
- Volume II reads those forms under pressure
- Great Books function as evidence
- Secret History enters selectively by function
- source-lattice preserves the source floor before widened interpretation
