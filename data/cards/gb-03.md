# Great Books #3: Poets and Prophets

## Where This Sits

This lecture places poets and prophets together as rival and overlapping voices that disclose worlds, found communities, and give language to invisible or sacred experience.

## Reading Posture

Read it as a comparison of literary and prophetic imagination, with the emphasis on how language creates public meaning.

## Historical Pressure Points

- Poetry and prophecy are treated as forms of world-making rather than private expression alone.
- The chapter links sacred speech, literary vision, authority, and communal memory.
- It helps bridge Great Books reading with the religious and civilizational arcs already present elsewhere in the corpus.

## Limits of the Frame

This entry is in review. Claims about prophets, sacred authority, religious transmission, and poetic world-making need source-layer distinction and interfaith caution.

## Return Path

Return here when comparing literary imagination with religious formation, prophetic speech, canon formation, or later Dante and biblical-material chapters.
