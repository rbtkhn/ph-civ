# Civilization #42: The Protestant Reformation and the Birth of Capitalism

## Where This Sits

This chapter moves the European sequence from Renaissance imagination into religious rupture and economic modernity. Protestantism is framed as direct access to God that breaks Catholic mediation, creates anxiety, and helps generate the discipline and belief structure Jiang associates with capitalism.

## Reading Posture

Read it as a strong synthesis chapter. Its value is in showing how theology, psychology, economy, and social order are being tied together; its risk is that the causal chain can become too compressed if treated as final history.

## Historical Pressure Points

- Catholic mediation is contrasted with Protestant direct access to Scripture and God.
- Anxiety over genuine faith becomes the chapter's psychological engine.
- Work, money, discipline, and signs of election are linked to capitalism.
- Weber and Durkheim provide the frame for capitalism, disconnection, suicide, and theocratic reaction.

## Limits of the Frame

This entry is in review after initial commentary analysis. The placement is strong, but Reformation-to-capitalism causation, Weber and Durkheim readings, suicide claims, and predictions about theocracy must remain bounded pending external review.

## Return Path

Return here when later chapters discuss capitalism, modern anxiety, Protestant influence, secularized religion, economic discipline, mass society, or religious reaction to modern disconnection.
